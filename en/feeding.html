Nutrition & wellbeing

Feeding & nutrition

The right food is one of the greatest gifts you can give your dog — and the easiest to get right.

The basics: premium kibble vs BARF

There is no single "best" diet — there is the one that suits your dog, your lifestyle, and your budget. What we can say with certainty: ingredient quality comes before everything else. A Nordic dog fed on budget supermarket kibble will pay the price in coat quality, energy, and lifespan.

Kibble — our recommendation

For our breeds, we recommend grain-free kibble or kibble with ancestral grains (millet, quinoa), with a real meat content above 60%. Nordic breeds have a high metabolism and a digestive system sensitive to refined grains.

Vet-approved brands: Orijen, Acana, Taste of the Wild, Canagan, Schesir Air Dry. Avoid supermarket brands — the ingredient list tells the whole story at a glance.

BARF — raw feeding

BARF (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food) means feeding your dog raw meat, meaty bones, offal, and vegetables. It is the diet closest to an animal's natural needs. Benefits: glossy coat, less waste, clean teeth. Constraints: preparation time, storage, strict nutritional balance required.

If you want to switch to BARF, do it gradually over 3 weeks — ideally with guidance from a veterinary nutritionist.

Strictly forbidden foods

  • Chocolate — toxic even in small quantities (theobromine)
  • Grapes & raisins — acute kidney failure; even a single grape can kill a small dog
  • Onion, garlic, shallot — red blood cell destruction
  • Xylitol (sweetener in chewing gums, some peanut butters) — sudden hypoglycaemia
  • Avocado — toxic persin compound
  • Cooked bones — shatter into splinters that can perforate the intestine
  • Macadamia nuts — temporary paralysis
  • Alcohol — obvious but worth stating

Nordic breed specifics

Pomsky and Husky have an extraordinary metabolism — they can function on less food than their size suggests. An under-stimulated, over-fed Husky will gain weight quickly. Monitor body condition (ribs should be palpable but not visible) rather than fixating on bowl quantities.
