The Pomeranian

Favourite of queens and tsarinas — a lion in a plush coat, pocket-sized.

Royal origins

From the Arctic Circle
to royal courts

The Pomeranian takes its name from Pomerania, a coastal region shared today between Germany and Poland. A descendant of the Nordic Spitz dogs that pulled sleds and herded livestock, it was progressively miniaturised to become the favourite companion of crowned heads across Europe.

Queen Victoria of England was famously devoted to the breed — she owned up to 35 Pomeranians. It is largely thanks to her influence that the breed spread throughout Europe and the size continued to decrease to the 1.5–3kg we know today.

Historical anecdote: Two Pomeranians are among the rare dogs to have survived the sinking of the Titanic in 1912.

A big dog in a small body

Don't be fooled by the size: the Pomeranian is an active, alert, and sometimes recklessly bold dog. It will not hesitate to challenge dogs ten times its size — a self-confidence that is admirable (and requires early socialisation).

The magnificent coat

The Pomeranian's double coat is its signature. The dense, woolly undercoat is covered by a long, straight outer coat that forms a spectacular ruff around the neck. It requires brushing two to three times per week, with extra attention during the two annual moults.
