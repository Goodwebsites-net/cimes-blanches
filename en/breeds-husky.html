The Siberian Husky

Born in the Siberian tundra, shaped over millennia alongside humans — the wolf that chose family.

Origins

Child of the Siberian ice

The Siberian Husky is one of the oldest breeds in the world. It was developed by the Chukchi people of northeastern Siberia more than 3,000 years ago. Its role: pull sleds over vast distances in temperatures of –60°C, while living in packs inside the family yurts at night.

It is this close cohabitation with humans over millennia that explains the breed's gentle, non-dominant temperament — contrary to what its wolf-like appearance might suggest. A Husky is not a guard dog. It is a partner.

Legendary feat: In 1925, teams of Huskies covered 1,000 km in 5 days to deliver a diphtheria serum to Nome, Alaska. The lead dog Balto is immortalised in a statue in Central Park.

A dog made for the active family

The Husky is a born athlete. It needs a minimum of two hours of exercise per day — a non-negotiable requirement. In return, you will have a companion of absolute loyalty, gentle with children, sociable with other dogs, and strikingly beautiful.

The bicolour gaze

The Husky's eyes are perhaps its most striking feature. Blue, brown, green, or heterochromia (one of each) — every Husky carries a unique gaze. Genetically, this variety is linked to a mutation in the ALX4 gene, which affects pigment concentration in the iris.
